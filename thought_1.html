The tragedy of the Kerala floods: A case for secession?

august 18,2018 ; 3:27 pm

The devastating tragedy of flood-hit Kerala – where over a hundred lives have been lost and lakhs have been rendered homeless – has deepened the divide between the India which lies south of the Vindhyas and the India which lies north of that mountain range.

There has long been a cultural and social divergence of views between the north and the south. Northeners routinely refer pejoratively to all people from the south as ‘Madrasis’. Southerners are hostile to what they perceive as the north’s real or imagined agenda of ‘Hindifying’ the south.

The Kerala floods have brought such divisiveness to the fore.

The Centre originally allotted Rs 100 crore for the Kerala Chief Minister’s Relief Fund, a sum which is less than the aid being given by several foreign countries, before belatedly, and still insufficiently, raising the sum to Rs 500 crore; a pitiful amount considering the initial estimated damage of Rs 8,500 crore suffered by the state.

While individuals from all over India have come forward to donate to the relief fund, social media in the north is allegedly abuzz with a spate of hate mail against Kerala, which has significant Muslim and Christian populations, and so worships the ‘wrong gods’.

Are such reports of hate mail fake news, or are they true? But the fact that they are in circulation suggests that there is a causative provocation for them.

Every now and then the question crops up if the south wouldn’t be economically and socially better off if it seceded from the north.

That question now seems to have been rendered irrelevant. For the simple reason that, in the unconscionably callous treatment meted out to Kerala by the Central government, the north, for the most part, seems to have seceded from the south.
