Nature’s fury: Unceasing rains, brimming dams worsen Kerala’s misery

august 11,2018 ; 11:00 am

The abnormally high rainfall that Kerala has received this year has led to over two dozen deaths in landslides and flash floods as well as the opening of sluice gates of 24 dams. This is a wakeup call on the dangers posed by extreme weather phenomena. Last August, Kerala was looking at a drought situation with 29% deficiency in rainfall, and planning water conservation measures. The situation stands dramatically reversed now with the state receiving 19% surplus rainfall and six of 14 districts recording 20-59% surplus rainfall.

Kerala government took time to factor concerns of dam safety, storing enough water for the lean season, moving people to relief camps, and identifying areas downstream on the Periyar river which were likely to flood before deciding to open the Idukki hydel project sluice gates under duress. This was a double whammy for people recovering from an earlier round of flooding just weeks ago. Kerala’s rivers drain rapidly thanks to the steady slope from Western Ghats to Arabian Sea, due to which floods are rare occurrences. But with rising sea levels, another consequence of climate change, Kerala’s vulnerability to floods has increased.



Across India, governments have been remiss in preserving flood plains, wetlands and water bodies that soak in excess rainwater. In Kerala, tackling the specific challenges of balancing development and preservation of Western Ghats, river banks, and coastal and estuarine mangroves from further encroachment cannot be delayed any further. Extreme weather phenomena – the high frequency of pre-monsoon dust and thunderstorms in north India this year are worth recalling – are here to stay. Fine tuning disaster response strategies, augmenting human capacity and auditing unsafe structures – buildings, dams, bridges – can restrict damage. Alongside, long-term climate change mitigation strategies must be undertaken as well.
